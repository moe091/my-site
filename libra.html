Libra - Educational Android Game

Tech:
Android, Java, AndEngine, Box2D, OpenGL

Libra was one of my first real freelance projects. The client was an educator who already had a game app that they designed created by another freelancer. The app that they had was made with OpenGL, but didn't function reliably and was missing some key features they wanted, so they decided to find another developer to remake the game in AndEngine and fix the parts that were lacking in their previous app. AndEngine was a popular 2d Android game engine at the time that worked well with Box2D.

This game was designed to teach fractions to young students. It consists of a balance scale and a bunch of small weights with a fraction drawn on them to denote how much they weight. At the beginning of a round, one side of the balance would have 1 or more weights on it and the other side would be empty. The player has a selection of different weights at the bottom of the screen and their goal is to drag and drop them on to the empty side of the scale to even out the weight. The game uses Box2D for real-time physics simulation, so the balance will tilt towards whichever side has more weight and even out as the user adds weights to the other side and gets closer to balancing the scale. The realistic physics and smooth feel helped students get an intuitive understanding of how fractions work and how the fractional weights relate to one another, I think the game really would help students comprehension of fractions.

The original version of the app was created with openGL, and didn't have real physics simulation but instead was just programmed to either tilt the scale left or right depending which side had more weight until the user evened out both sides. In addition to providing the inuitive understanding of fractions, adding the physics also made the app function much more smoothly - e.g. instead of draging a weight to one side of the scale or the other and having it 'snap' into position on one of the scale plates, users could now drag it around the drop it and let it fall due to gravity and either land on the scale or fall to the ground, and users had to physically drop the weights on to the scale for it to work. If the user dropped the weight from a high height onto the scale it would make the scale quickly tip in the direction of the dropped weight before oscilating back and forth a few times and then balancing out - just as you'd expect the balance to behave in real life. The realistic feel made the game much more fun to play with in my opinion and probably helped engage students much more than the previous version of the app.

When I took the job I had almost no Android development experience, but it was a porting job from OpenGL to AndEngine, and I already knew a very small amount of OpenGL and had heard that AndEngine was easy to use and learn, so I decided to write a proposal for the job and hope the client hired me anyway. To my surprise I got hired and only had a couple weeks to complete the project - which in hindsight is a pretty long time for an App like this, but since I had to learn Android development and AndEngine first it was kind of a tight deadline for me. I finished the project with only a couple days to spare, and managed to fix all the bugs and missing features in the OpenGL version of the game(mostly just a bug to do with scoring and a problem with the touch & drag functionality not working sometimes) as well as add the realistic physics in and make the gameplay much smoother, which the client was very happy with.
